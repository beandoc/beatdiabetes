Exercise affects blood glucose differently depending on type, duration, and intensity. Plan your activity safely.

🍌

Pre-Exercise Snack

If BG is 90–120 mg/dL before aerobic exercise, eat 15–30g fast-acting carbs (banana, glucose tabs, juice). No bolus needed for this snack.

⏰

Timing Matters

Avoid exercising at peak insulin activity (1–3 hrs after bolus). Morning exercise with lower insulin-on-board is safer for most T1D athletes.

🌙

Overnight Hypoglycemia

Late-afternoon/evening exercise increases overnight hypo risk. Have a 15–30g complex carb snack before bed. Set a CGM low alarm at 80 mg/dL.

💡 Adjunct Medications in T1D (Evidence-Based)
💊

Metformin

Used off-label in T1D for insulin resistance. May reduce insulin dose requirements. Common in overweight T1D patients. Discuss with your doctor.

💉

Pramlintide (Symlin)

Amylin analog injected with meals. Slows gastric emptying, reduces post-meal spikes, may reduce insulin needs. Can cause nausea initially.

🫀

SGLT-2 Inhibitors

Dapagliflozin/Sotagliflozin used off-label in T1D. Reduces glucose excretion, lowers A1C. DKA risk is higher — requires strict monitoring.

🦋

Levothyroxine

Autoimmune thyroid disease (Hashimoto's) affects up to 30% of T1D patients. Thyroid function should be tested annually.

🌿

Vitamin D & Magnesium

Deficiencies are common in T1D. Supplementation may improve insulin sensitivity. Get levels tested at annual review.

🫁

Celiac Disease

Affects 5–10% of T1D patients. Gluten-free diet significantly improves glucose control and intestinal absorption. Annual screening recommended.

🍽️ T1D-Friendly Dietary Strategies
🥗

Low-Carb / Ketogenic

Reduces carb intake to 20–100g/day. Can dramatically reduce insulin requirements and glucose variability. Requires careful medical supervision and insulin adjustment.

🌊

Mediterranean Diet

Emphasises olive oil, fish, legumes, and vegetables. Lower GI foods reduce post-meal spikes. Associated with improved cardiovascular outcomes in T1D.

🌿

Plate Method

Fill ½ plate with non-starchy veg, ¼ with lean protein, ¼ with complex carbs. Naturally limits carb intake and simplifies carb counting at each meal.

⏱️

Consistent Meal Timing

Eating at consistent times daily helps stabilise basal-bolus rhythms. Irregular meal timing is one of the most common causes of unpredictable BG patterns.

🫘

High Fibre Choices

Soluble fiber (oats, beans, lentils) slows glucose absorption. Aim for 25–35g of fiber daily. High-fiber meals may require less bolus or later injection timing.

🚫

Foods to Limit

Sugary drinks, white bread, white rice, processed snacks, and alcohol cause rapid BG spikes. Alcohol can also cause delayed hypoglycemia up to 12–24 hrs after drinking.

🍺 Alcohol & Blood Glucose — Important Guide
⚠️ Alcohol Warning: Alcohol blocks gluconeogenesis (liver's ability to release glucose). This can cause severe delayed hypoglycemia 4–12 hours after drinking. Never drink on an empty stomach.
⚠️ Risks
  • Masks hypoglycemia symptoms
  • Inhibits liver glucose release
  • Impairs judgement for dosing
  • Dehydration worsens glucose control
  • Some drinks are very high in carbs
✅ Safer Strategies
  • Always eat before/during drinking
  • Reduce or skip your bolus for mixed drinks
  • Set overnight CGM alarm to 90 mg/dL
  • Wear medical ID alert
  • Inform a trusted person about your T1D
  • Check BG before bed; eat complex carbs
🤒 Sick Day Protocol
Illness raises blood glucose and increases DKA risk even if you can't eat. Never stop insulin when sick.
📋 Step-by-Step Sick Day Rules
1

Never Stop Insulin

Continue all insulin — even if you can't eat. Illness hormones (cortisol, glucagon) raise BG. You may actually need more insulin, not less.

2

Check BG Every 2–4 Hours

Monitor more frequently than normal. Check for patterns. If consistently >250 mg/dL despite corrections, check ketones immediately.

3

Check Ketones

Check urine or blood ketones every 4 hours if BG >250 mg/dL. Blood ketone meters are more accurate. Moderate/large ketones = call your doctor or go to ER.

4

Stay Hydrated

Drink 8+ oz of fluid every hour. If unable to eat, drink sugar-containing fluids (juice, ginger ale, sports drinks) to prevent hypoglycemia. Avoid regular sodas in excess.

5

Follow Sick-Day Carb Targets

If you can't keep food down, aim for 15–50g carbs every 3–4 hours. Choose easy foods: broth, crackers, popsicles, toast, apple juice.

6

When to Call Your Doctor

Call if: BG >300 mg/dL for more than 2 readings; moderate/large ketones; vomiting >2 times; unable to keep fluids down; severe abdominal pain; confusion or extreme fatigue.

🚨 Go to the ER immediately if you have vomiting + ketones + BG >250 mg/dL. This is a DKA emergency.

🔬 Complications & Screening Schedule
Regular screenings catch complications early, when they're most treatable. Use this as a reference for your annual care plan.
📅 Recommended Screening Schedule
👁️

Diabetic Retinopathy — Eye Exam

Dilated retinal exam to detect retinopathy, macular oedema, or other changes. Early detection prevents vision loss.

Annually
🫁

Nephropathy — Kidney Function

Urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio + eGFR. Microalbuminuria is the earliest sign of diabetic kidney disease.

Annually
🦶

Neuropathy — Foot Examination

Comprehensive foot exam including vibration, monofilament testing, and vascular assessment. Daily self-inspection of feet.

Annually (or more)
🫀

Cardiovascular — Lipid Panel + BP

Total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides, blood pressure, and resting ECG. T1D is a major CV risk factor.

Annually
🦋

Thyroid — TSH Test

Hashimoto's thyroiditis is 3× more common in T1D. Hypothyroidism worsens glucose control and cholesterol levels.

Every 1–2 years
🩻

Celiac Disease — tTG-IgA Antibodies

Affects 5–10% of T1D patients. Undiagnosed celiac causes malabsorption and unpredictable BG patterns.

Every 2 years
🦷

Dental — Periodontal Check

High glucose impairs immune response, increasing infection and periodontal disease risk. Gum disease also worsens glycaemic control.

Bi-annually
🧠

Mental Health Screening

Depression affects 2–3× more T1D patients vs. general population. Diabetes distress and burnout screening are part of standard care.

🧠 Mental Health & Diabetes Distress
Living with T1D is a 24/7 demanding condition. Mental health is an essential — not optional — part of diabetes care.
📋 Diabetes Distress Screener (DDS-4)

Rate each item from 1 (no problem) to 6 (a serious problem) over the past month.

💬 Common Psychological Challenges in T1D
😓

Diabetes Distress

Emotional burden specific to managing diabetes — fear of complications, burnout, frustration with glucose variability. Affects 40–50% of T1D patients.

😰

Hypoglycemia Fear

Excessive anxiety about low glucose leads some to run BG high deliberately. A specific fear that needs targeted cognitive therapy — not just reassurance.

😞

Depression

Depression is 2–3× more common in T1D. Worsens self-care and glucose control. Screening with PHQ-9 at every visit is recommended by ADA.

🍽️

Disordered Eating

"Diabulimia" (deliberate insulin omission for weight loss) is a serious but underrecognised issue in T1D, especially in young women. Requires specialist care.

🔥

Burnout

Feeling overwhelmed and disengaged from diabetes management. Common after years of intensive management. Talking to a diabetes educator or psychologist helps.

💪

Building Resilience

Peer support groups, acceptance-based therapy, CGM data review, and realistic goal-setting are proven strategies for long-term psychological wellbeing in T1D.

🌿 Coping Strategies & Self-Care
Evidence-Based Strategies
Problem-solving therapy — breaking management challenges into small, solvable steps
Mindfulness-based stress reduction — reduces cortisol which directly raises blood glucose
Peer support groups — JDRF, Beyond Type 1, and online communities offer connection and practical advice
Regular exercise — proven antidepressant; improves insulin sensitivity and mood simultaneously
Scheduled "diabetes holidays" — brief planned breaks from data obsession (not from insulin) under guidance
🗣️ Ask your doctor for a referral to a diabetes-specialised psychologist or social worker. Mental health support is part of standard T1D care, not a "last resort."

✈️ Travel with T1D
Travel requires advance preparation. With the right plan, T1D should never stop you from exploring the world.
🎒 Pre-Travel Preparation Checklist
📋
Medical Documentation: Carry a letter from your endocrinologist listing all medications, devices, and your diagnosis. Get it translated if travelling abroad. This is essential for airport security and customs.
💊
Double Your Supplies: Pack 2× your usual insulin, syringes/pen needles, CGM sensors, pump supplies, and lancets. Split between carry-on and checked luggage. Never check all insulin — it can freeze in cargo.
❄️
Insulin Storage: Use an insulated travel case (FRIO wallet, Medicool). Opened insulin stays viable at room temperature up to 28–30 days. Unopened vials must be refrigerated (2–8°C). Never let insulin freeze or overheat (>37°C).
🛂
Airport Security: Inform TSA/security about your diabetes supplies. Insulin pens, pumps, CGMs, and lancets are allowed through security. Request hand-inspection instead of X-ray for insulin pumps if needed. Keep all supplies in original packaging if possible.
🌍
Time Zone Changes: For flights crossing multiple time zones, adjust long-acting insulin doses with your endocrinologist's guidance. On pump therapy, update time zone settings only after arriving at destination. Check BG frequently during and after travel.
🍽️
Unfamiliar Foods: When eating unknown foods, wait and watch — bolus conservatively, check BG 1 and 2 hrs after the meal, and adjust for future similar meals. Learn carb counts for common local dishes in advance.
🏖️
Heat & Humidity: High temperatures reduce insulin absorption speed and can degrade insulin faster. Check BG more frequently at the beach or in hot climates. Keep insulin cool and out of direct sunlight.
🆘
Emergency Contacts: Save your endocrinologist's number. Research the nearest hospital or diabetes clinic at your destination. Know how to say "I have Type 1 Diabetes" and "I need sugar" in the local language.

🚨 Emergency Quick Reference

HYPOGLYCEMIA — BG < 70 mg/dL

  • Eat 15g fast carbs immediately
  • Wait 15 minutes — recheck BG
  • Repeat if still <70 mg/dL
  • Eat a snack once BG >100 mg/dL
  • If unconscious: Glucagon injection
  • Call 112 / 911 if unresponsive
🍬 15g = 4 glucose tabs · 4 oz juice · 6 crackers · 1 tbsp honey

HYPERGLYCEMIA / DKA WARNING SIGNS

  • BG persistently >250 mg/dL
  • Moderate or large ketones (urine/blood)
  • Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
  • Fruity-smelling breath
  • Rapid or laboured breathing
  • Extreme fatigue or confusion
  • Excessive thirst & urination
📞 Go to ER immediately — DKA is life-threatening

SEVERE HYPOGLYCEMIA RESPONSE

  • Do NOT give food/drink if unconscious
  • Inject Glucagon (kit or Baqsimi nasal)
  • Turn person on their side
  • Call emergency services
  • Stay until help arrives
💉 Gvoke autoinjector: 0.5mg (<45kg) or 1mg (≥45kg)

  • Emergency: 112
  • Ambulance: 102 / 108
  • Vandrevala (mental health): 1860-2662-345
